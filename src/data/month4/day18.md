I clicked my small, dim flashlight on, staring nervously down into the dark, echoing tunnel entrance of the famous Paris Catacombs. As a curious history student, I desperately wanted to explore the hidden, forbidden underground passages that held millions of carefully stacked, ancient human bones. I foolishly ignored the clear warning signs and confidently stepped off the safe, marked path directly into the total, suffocating darkness. I walked carefully through the wet, narrow limestone corridors, marveling at the beautifully arranged skulls and massive piles of dusty femurs. I turned multiple confusing, identical corners, eventually realizing that the twisting paths had completely scrambled my internal sense of direction. My small flashlight suddenly flickered rapidly and died completely, plunging me into absolute, terrifying, silent darkness deep beneath the bustling, noisy city. I desperately blindly touched the cold, wet bone walls, moving slowly through the tight spaces while cold, freezing water dripped loudly onto my shaking shoulders. Panic gripped my chest tightly, and my throat burned as my rapid, shallow breathing echoed loudly in the incredibly silent, empty space. After many exhausting, terrifying hours of wandering blindly, I finally spotted a tiny, faint glimmer of yellow light down a long, narrow tunnel. I rushed eagerly toward the glowing exit and burst happily out into the cool, fresh evening air of a quiet, empty street. I collapsed onto the dirty, wet pavement, incredibly grateful to see the bright, sparkling city lights above me. That terrifying, underground ordeal strongly reminded me that there is a genuine, important reason certain boundaries and rules are put in place.
