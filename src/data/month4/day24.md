I shivered uncontrollably inside the thick, borrowed parka, standing on the dark, freezing shores of a completely empty beach in Iceland. I had traveled eagerly to this incredibly remote, freezing island specifically to photograph the legendary, magnificent Northern Lights dancing across the pitch-black sky. My expensive camera was mounted securely on a sturdy tripod, but thick, grey clouds stubbornly covered the stars, deeply frustrating my incredibly high, optimistic hopes. I waited patiently for several agonizing, freezing hours, drinking hot, sweet tea from a metal thermos, which is an insulated container used to keep liquids warm. A wise, local fisherman slowly approached my lonely spot, quietly explaining that the beautiful lights often require true, profound patience and a genuine appreciation for the stark, empty landscape itself. He gently pointed toward the distant, dark horizon, urging me to simply lower my camera and watch carefully with my own two eyes. The thick, grey clouds suddenly parted rapidly, but the incredibly bright, green light did not come from the dark sky, rather it began glowing strangely directly beneath the freezing, crashing ocean waves. My jaw dropped completely open, and a thrilling, electric shock of pure wonder rushed quickly down my spine at the completely impossible, bizarre sight. I quickly grabbed my heavy camera and excitedly snapped dozens of sharp, incredible photos of the strange, glowing bioluminescent plankton crashing beautifully against the cold, black rocks. Suddenly, the magnificent, green Northern Lights finally began dancing wildly in the clearing sky, perfectly mirroring the bright, magical ocean below. I returned happily to my warm, cozy hotel room, reviewing the most unique, spectacular photographs I had ever successfully captured. The freezing, magical night beautifully demonstrated that true, natural wonder frequently appears when we stop desperately forcing our own narrow, specific expectations on the unpredictable world.
