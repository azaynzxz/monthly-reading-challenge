I nervously patted the front pocket of my heavy winter coat while standing alone on the busy, bright platform of the massive Tokyo train station. I was scheduled to quickly board my very long, international flight home in exactly three hours, but my crucial, essential passport had completely vanished from my bag. Absolute panic instantly washed thoroughly over me, quickly realizing I might be permanently stuck in a foreign, overwhelming country with absolutely no money or valid identification. I frantically retraced my incredibly hurried, messy steps through the crowded, neon-lit streets, returning to the small, quiet ramen shop where I had happily eaten lunch. The incredibly polite, apologetic owner bowed deeply but explicitly confirmed he had absolutely not seen my small, dark blue passport booklet anywhere near my table. I then desperately sprinted toward a distant, crowded municipal park, hoping I had simply dropped it somewhere on the quiet, snowy path by the pond. A strict, uniform-wearing police officer suddenly stopped me near the park entrance, seriously asking for my missing, completely lost identification documents. My fast-beating heart leaped painfully into my dry throat, and my shaking hands sweated profusely as I stammered, entirely unable to communicate my terrible, scary situation in fluent Japanese. A kind, observant local teenager slowly approached and perfectly translated my frantic, desperate explanation to the highly suspicious, stern police officer. Incredibly, the officer smiled warmly and quickly pulled my missing, blue passport directly from his front pocket, explaining that a young child had recently found it safely by the pond. I happily made my international flight with literally only minutes to spare, collapsing entirely exhausted into my comfortable airplane seat. The incredibly stressful, terrifying emergency beautifully taught me that complete strangers can consistently be incredibly kind and deeply honest, no matter where you are in the vast world.
