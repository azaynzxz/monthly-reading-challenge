I heard the thunderous roar of the waterfall long before I could actually see it with my own eyes, and the sound grew louder and more powerful with every step I took along the winding forest path beside the mighty Zambezi River. The local Tokaleya Tonga people who have lived in this region for countless generations call it Mosi-oa-Tunya, which translates to The Smoke that Thunders in their native language. The Scottish explorer David Livingstone was the first European to see this wonder in 1855, and he named it Victoria Falls after his queen, but the original African name captures its essence far better. They gave it this name because the spray from the falls rises so high into the air that it looks exactly like columns of smoke rising from a great fire. When I finally emerged from the trees and saw that massive curtain of white water dropping dramatically into the deep canyon below, I was completely and utterly speechless. Adventurous visitors can swim in a natural pool at the very edge of the falls called the Devil's Pool, though I was not brave enough to try it myself. It is one of the largest waterfalls in the world, and witnessing its raw power in person is a humbling experience that photographs simply cannot capture. The mist created by the crashing water is so thick and constant that it creates permanent rainbows that can be seen for many miles around on sunny days. The incredible sound of millions of liters of water crashing down every second vibrates right through your chest and makes your heart beat faster. Standing there, I felt small in the presence of such magnificent natural power, and I was reminded of how truly incredible our planet really is.
