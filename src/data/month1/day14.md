My grandmother called me over for coffee this afternoon, and I knew immediately that I was in for something special because this tradition comes from the very birthplace of coffee itself. She sat on a low wooden stool near the doorway, and she carefully roasted those green coffee beans herself over a small charcoal fire until they turned dark and shiny. The rich, aromatic smoke filled every corner of the room, and it smelled exactly like home and childhood memories all wrapped into one comforting embrace. After the beans were perfectly roasted, she ground them by hand using a mortar and pestle, and then she brewed the coffee slowly in a traditional clay pot called a jebena. The entire process took nearly an hour, but that is precisely the point of this sacred ritual. Drinking three cups is the custom in our culture, and each cup has its own traditional name and significance. The first cup is called Abol, and it is the strongest because the coffee grounds are fresh and full of flavor. The second cup is called Tona, and this is the time when we share stories and have meaningful conversations with one another. The third and final cup is called Baraka, which means blessing, and drinking this cup is believed to bring good fortune and spiritual peace to all who share it. This beautiful ceremony teaches us patience and the value of slowing down, and it gives us the precious time we need to really talk about life, share our worries, and strengthen the bonds between generations.
