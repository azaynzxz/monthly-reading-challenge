I walked slowly down the famous Allee des Baobabs, which means Avenue of the Baobabs in French, near the town of Morondava on the western coast of Madagascar. This dusty red dirt road is lined on both sides with enormous trees that look like they are growing upside down, with their thick branches seemingly sticking straight up into the sky like roots. The Malagasy people call these magnificent trees renala, which means mother of the forest, and they consider them sacred according to their traditional beliefs called fady. These Baobab trees are affectionately known worldwide as the Tree of Life because of their remarkable ability to store thousands of liters of water inside their massive trunks in order to survive the long and harsh drought seasons. Some of these ancient giants are believed to be over eight hundred years old, and they have stood as silent witnesses to countless generations of human history. As the sun began to set in the west, the thick silhouettes of these extraordinary trees stood out dramatically against the burning orange and purple sky, and the scene looked absolutely magical and otherworldly. I sat down on a fallen log and just watched in awe as the colors of the sky slowly shifted and changed. The local people use almost every part of the Baobab tree for food, medicine, and shelter, and they make a nutritious powder from the fruit that is rich in vitamin C. These remarkable trees teach us valuable lessons about the importance of preparation and resilience, and they show us that even in the harshest of climates, life can find a way to thrive and endure for centuries.
