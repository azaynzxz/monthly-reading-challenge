The towering cliffs of Bamiyan are a stunning burnt orange color that contrasts beautifully against the clear blue sky above. Even though the famous giant Buddha statues that once stood here are now gone, the enormous empty niches carved into the rock face still remain standing like giant shadows that remind visitors of what once was. The larger statue called Salsal was 55 meters tall, and the smaller one called Shamama was 38 meters tall, and they were tragically destroyed in 2001. I spent several hours exploring the ancient caves that are carved into the cliffs, and I tried to imagine what life was like for the Buddhist monks who used to live and meditate in these very chambers centuries ago. The local Hazara people, who have distinct Central Asian features, welcomed me warmly and shared stories about their ancestors. During that period, this valley was an important center of Buddhism and a major stopping point along the famous Silk Road trade route that connected East and West. Not far from here are the stunning Band-e-Amir lakes, which are a series of six brilliant blue lakes that became Afghanistan's first national park. This remarkable place is filled with deep history and represents the incredible resilience and ability to recover from tragedy that defines the human spirit. The valley itself is surprisingly green and full of life, with fields of crops and orchards growing along the river below. The community here continues to move forward with hope and determination despite the scars of the past, and their strength is truly inspiring to witness.
