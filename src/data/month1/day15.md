We drove to Kandahar early this morning, and the journey took us through endless orchards that stretched as far as the eye could see. The trees were full of heavy fruit called anar in our local language, and they hung from the branches like precious jewels waiting to be picked. We stopped at a small roadside stand to buy these famous Kandahari pomegranates, which are considered a powerful symbol of prosperity and good fortune in our culture, especially during Nowruz, which is the Persian New Year celebration. Many people believe that the pomegranates grown in this region are among the finest in the entire world, and some farmers dry the seeds to make anardana, which is a tangy spice used in cooking. I cracked open the tough, leathery skin of one fruit, and it revealed hundreds of shining ruby-red seeds that were packed tightly inside like tiny treasures. The taste was extraordinary because the seeds were both sweet and tart at the same time, and they exploded with refreshing juice that ran down my chin. We sat together under the shade of a tree, and we shared this delicious fruit with my family while the sun slowly began to set over the Hindu Kush mountains. Sharing this simple pleasure with the people I love reminds me of the richness of our land and the importance of appreciating the small gifts that nature provides us every single day.
