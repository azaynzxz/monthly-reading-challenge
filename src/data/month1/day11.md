They call this place Kota Hujan, which means the City of Rain in Indonesian, because it experiences frequent and powerful lightning storms that roll in almost every afternoon without fail. I arrived here early in the day when the sky was still clear and bright, and I spent the morning exploring the famous Kebun Raya, which is a massive botanical garden established during the Dutch colonial era. However, the dark clouds began gathering quickly as the afternoon approached, just as the locals had warned me they would. Within just a few minutes, the entire sky opened up with a tremendous roar of thunder, and the rain came pouring down in heavy sheets. The sound of that powerful rain hitting the large tropical leaves all around me was absolutely deafening, and yet it was also incredibly soothing to my ears. I took shelter on a wooden porch of a traditional house, and I sat there drinking wedang jahe, which is a warm and spicy ginger drink that is perfect for rainy weather. The air filled with petrichor, which is that wonderful fresh scent of wet earth rising up from the ground after the rain begins to fall. I closed my eyes and breathed it in deeply, feeling completely at peace with the world despite the chaos of the storm. The rain washes away all the dust and heat that accumulates during the dry days, and it leaves everything looking green, fresh, and wonderfully alive once the clouds finally clear. This is nature's way of renewing the land, and I am grateful to witness it.
