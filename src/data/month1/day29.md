Many people around the world often think that this legendary place is just a myth or a figure of speech used to describe somewhere impossibly remote, but I actually stood there with my own two feet today and saw it with my own eyes. Timbuktu was once a thriving center of learning and intellectual achievement during the 15th and 16th centuries, and it rose to prominence after the famous Emperor Mansa Musa passed through on his legendary pilgrimage to Mecca, spreading gold and establishing the city's reputation across the world. The ancient Sankore University attracted scholars from across Africa and the Islamic world who would travel here to study and exchange knowledge. I was given the rare privilege of visiting the Ahmed Baba Institute, which is named after a famous scholar from the city, and I saw remarkable manuscripts that were written carefully by hand many centuries ago. These precious documents cover an astonishing range of subjects including astronomy, mathematics, law, medicine, and philosophy, and they represent an incredible wealth of African scholarship that many people do not know exists. The Tuareg people, who are known as the blue people because the indigo dye from their traditional robes stains their skin, still travel through this region as their ancestors did for centuries along the ancient trade routes. I also visited the magnificent Djinguereber Mosque, which was built from mud bricks in 1327 and still stands as a testament to the incredible building techniques of that era. Seeing these ancient texts and structures with my own eyes was a powerful and emotional experience, and it reminded me that knowledge and high-quality education have deep and strong roots in African history that stretch back for many hundreds of years. We have a sacred duty to protect and preserve these irreplaceable treasures for future generations so that they can learn about and take pride in this rich intellectual heritage.
