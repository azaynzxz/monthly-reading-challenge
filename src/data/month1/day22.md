I absolutely could not believe my own eyes when I first saw Lake Retba stretching out before me this morning. The locals call it Lac Rose in French, which simply means the Pink Lake, and the water was actually a stunning shade of pink that looked exactly like a giant strawberry milkshake had been poured across the landscape. This remarkable color comes from a unique type of algae called Dunaliella salina that thrives in extremely salty environments and produces red pigments to absorb sunlight. This lake was once famous as the finishing point of the legendary Paris-Dakar Rally, which was one of the most challenging automobile races in the world. I stood at the edge of the lake and watched as local Wolof workers waded into the shallow waters to harvest salt from the sandy bottom below. The salt content of this remarkable lake is even higher than that of the famous Dead Sea, and the workers must coat their skin with karite, which is shea butter, to protect themselves from the harsh effects of the brine. Their skin glistened with sweat and protective oil as they worked tirelessly under the hot African sun, filling their wooden pirogues with the precious white salt. This extraordinary place demonstrates beautifully how nature can be both stunningly beautiful and incredibly useful at the same time, and it provides a sustainable livelihood for the local communities who have depended on its resources for generations.
