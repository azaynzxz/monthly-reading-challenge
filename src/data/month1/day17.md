I got completely lost in the winding narrow streets of the medina, which is the old city quarter of Chefchaouen, and I could not have been happier about the situation. Every single wall, door, staircase, and step in this remarkable town is painted in the most soothing shades of blue, and historians believe that Jewish refugees who settled here in the 1930s brought this tradition with them because blue symbolizes the sky and heaven in their culture. Walking through these azure pathways felt exactly like moving through a beautiful lucid dream where reality and fantasy blend together seamlessly. The morning light reflected off the blue surfaces and created a calm atmosphere that washed away all the stress I had been carrying. I found a quiet spot and sat down on a blue step near a small fountain, and I ate fresh warm khobz, which is traditional Moroccan bread, while I watched stray cats sleeping peacefully in the cool shade. An elderly man walked past me and smiled warmly, and he offered me a glass of atay bi nana, which is the famous Moroccan mint tea that is poured from a great height to create a frothy top. This incredibly photogenic and surreal place is like nowhere else on earth, and it reminded me that sometimes the most magical experiences come from getting lost and letting the journey unfold naturally.
