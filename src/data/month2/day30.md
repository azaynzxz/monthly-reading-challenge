I am standing on the dusty volcanic soil of Rinca Island, which is part of the Komodo National Park located off the coast of Flores in eastern Indonesia, and my heart is pounding with a mixture of excitement and healthy fear. Our park ranger carries a long forked stick as we walk carefully through the dry savanna landscape, and he explains that this simple wooden tool is our only protection against the massive reptiles we have come to see. The Komodo dragon is the largest living lizard on Earth, growing up to three meters long and weighing over 70 kilograms, and it has existed in this remote corner of Indonesia for millions of years, making it a living dinosaur. We do not have to wait long before we spot our first dragon basking in the shade of a tamarind tree, and it is even more impressive and terrifying than I imagined. Its forked yellow tongue flicks in and out constantly, tasting the air for the scent of prey, and its powerful claws dig into the earth as it watches us with ancient, unblinking eyes. The ranger tells us that Komodo dragons have bacteria in their saliva that can cause deadly infections, and they are ambush predators capable of taking down water buffalo and deer with a single venomous bite. Flores itself is a beautiful island of volcanic mountains, traditional villages, and the famous tri-colored crater lakes of Kelimutu, which change color depending on the mineral content and the position of the sun. The Manggarai people of Flores have their own unique culture and traditions, including the spectacular caci whip fighting ceremony and the distinctive cone-shaped roofs of their traditional houses. As I watch this prehistoric predator slowly rise and lumber away into the thorny forest, I feel incredibly privileged to witness one of nature's most extraordinary survivors in its wild and natural habitat.
