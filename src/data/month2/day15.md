I am sitting cross-legged on the floor around a large woven basket called a mesob in a small family home in Addis Ababa, which is the capital city of Ethiopia and sits high in the mountains at an altitude that makes the air cool and fresh. Through the window, I can see the groves of eucalyptus trees covering the distant hills in shades of silver and green. Inside, we are gathered around a giant platter covered with injera, which is a large, thin, and spongy flatbread that serves as both the plate and the utensil for our meal. This unique bread is made from teff flour, which comes from a tiny grain that grows only in the Ethiopian highlands and is one of the most nutritious grains on earth. We do not use forks, spoons, or knives because eating with your hands is the traditional and proper way to enjoy Ethiopian food. I tear off a piece of the slightly sour and beautifully textured bread and use it to scoop up the spicy lentil stew called misir wot, which is seasoned with berbere, a complex spice blend that includes fenugreek, cardamom, and many other aromatic ingredients. My friend sitting across from me reaches over and feeds me a large bite with his hand, and I am honored because this gesture is called gursha, which is a profound sign of love, respect, and friendship in Ethiopian culture. The more someone cares about you, the bigger the gursha they will give you. This beautiful tradition of eating from a shared plate reminds me that food always tastes better when it is enjoyed together with people you love.
