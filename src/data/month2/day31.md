It is the middle of October, and I am standing on a steep rocky hillside in the Ajloun region of northern Jordan, where the air smells of pine trees, wild herbs, and the rich dark earth that has nurtured olive groves for thousands of years. This part of Jordan is covered with rolling hills and green valleys that look nothing like the desert landscapes that most foreigners imagine when they think of the Middle East, and the scenery here reminds many visitors of Tuscany or southern Spain. My entire extended family has gathered today for the annual olive harvest, which is one of the most important events in the Jordanian agricultural calendar and a tradition that connects us to our ancestors who planted and tended these very same trees generations ago. Some of the olive trees in our family's grove are absolutely ancient, with twisted and gnarled trunks that suggest they have been growing here for three hundred years or more. The oldest trees are treated with special reverence because they have witnessed so much history and have fed so many generations of our family through wars, droughts, and times of both hardship and prosperity. We spread large canvas sheets on the ground beneath each tree, and then we use long wooden rakes and poles to gently shake the branches and knock the ripe olives loose. The olives fall like black and green rain, and they bounce and roll on the sheets below. It is hard physical work that makes your shoulders and back ache by the end of the day, but my uncle keeps us motivated by singing old folk songs that his father taught him many decades ago, and we all join in on the familiar choruses. At midday, we stop working and spread a simple picnic lunch under the shade of the largest tree in the grove. We eat fresh khubz flatbread that my aunt baked in a taboon oven this morning, and we dip pieces of the warm bread into small dishes of olive oil that was pressed last year from the very trees that surround us. The oil is deep green and slightly peppery, with a richness that comes only from olives that have been grown in this particular soil and pressed within hours of harvesting. We are not wealthy people by any modern standard, but sitting here with my family, eating bread and oil that we produced ourselves from trees that our great-great-grandparents planted, I feel like the richest person in the entire world.
