I am sitting on a low wooden stool on a dusty sidewalk in Khartoum, which is the capital city of Sudan and sits at the historic confluence where the White Nile and Blue Nile rivers join together to form the great Nile that flows all the way to the Mediterranean Sea. Not far from where I sit, I can see the murky waters of these two great rivers meeting, and the distinct colors of each river remain visible for some distance downstream before they finally merge together completely. However, my attention right now is focused on someone much more important to my daily life than any geographical wonder: the Tea Lady who sits before me on her own small stool. She is the true queen of this street corner, and she has been coming to this exact spot every day for over twenty years, arriving before the sun rises and leaving only after the last evening customers have gone home. She has her small charcoal stove glowing red with heat, and the coals crackle and pop as she adds small sticks to keep the temperature just right. With expert speed and confidence born of decades of practice, she mixes strong black tea with aromatic spices including ginger, cinnamon, and cardamom, and she adds generous amounts of sugar because sweetness is considered essential for proper hospitality in Sudanese culture. The metal spoon clinks rhythmically against the glass as she stirs, and the sound is as familiar to me as my mother's voice. University students with their heavy textbooks, office workers in their pressed shirts, construction laborers with dust on their clothes, and retired elders with long white beards all sit together on plastic crates and wooden stools around her tiny stove. They argue passionately about football matches and local politics, they share gossip about neighbors and celebrities, and they discuss the deep philosophical questions of existence. This is the most democratic and cheapest cafe in all of Khartoum, right here under the sprawling branches of an ancient Acacia tree, and the only price of admission is respect for the Tea Lady and appreciation for the simple pleasure of a well-made cup of tea.
