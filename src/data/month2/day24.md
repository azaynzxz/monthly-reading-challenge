I am sitting on the narrow balcony of my apartment in Medellin, which is known as the City of Eternal Spring because its location in a valley of the Colombian Andes gives it perfect weather throughout the entire year. I am looking up at the lush green mountains that embrace our city like protective arms, and a light mist is drifting down from the peaks this cool morning, creating a magical atmosphere that makes everything feel peaceful and new. In my hand is my breakfast: a perfectly grilled corn cake called an arepa that my mother prepared for me just minutes ago using techniques that have been passed down through our family for many generations. The arepa is one of the most ancient foods in the Americas, and indigenous peoples were making them long before Christopher Columbus arrived on these shores. My mother grilled this one on a traditional grill until it developed those characteristic black char marks on the outside, but when I break it open, the inside is beautifully soft and steaming hot. I place a thick slice of queso costeno, which is a salty white cheese from the Caribbean coast of Colombia, inside the arepa and watch as it begins to melt from the heat. Holding this warm and comforting corn cake on a cool mountain morning while the city slowly wakes up below me is one of the best feelings I know. It is such a humble and simple food, but it connects every Colombian to our shared history and to each other. Whether you are rich or poor, whether you live in a mansion or a small apartment like mine, the arepa is always present at our breakfast tables, and it reminds us that the most meaningful things in life are often the simplest ones.
