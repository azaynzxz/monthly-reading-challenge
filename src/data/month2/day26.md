It is Friday, which is the holy day of rest in Iran and the equivalent of Sunday in Western countries, and I have come with my extended family to the magnificent Naqsh-e Jahan Square in the heart of Isfahan, which is one of the largest and most beautiful public squares in the entire world. We have spread our colorful woven rug on the well-maintained grass under the shade of a grand Plane tree whose massive trunk suggests it has been growing here for hundreds of years. The spectacular blue tiled dome of the Shah Mosque rises at one end of the square, and its intricate geometric patterns seem to glow with an inner light as the late afternoon sun catches the glazed surfaces at just the right angle. This mosque was completed in 1629 during the golden age of Persian architecture, and the mathematical precision of its design still fills architects and mathematicians with wonder today. We are not the only family enjoying this perfect afternoon because hundreds of other groups are scattered across the grass with their own rugs, thermoses of hot tea, and baskets of food. Iranian people have a deep tradition of picnicking called doreh, and it is common for families to spend entire days relaxing outdoors together, especially during the mild spring and autumn months. I am eating fresh sangak flatbread that is still warm from the bakery, and I am dipping pieces into a bowl of creamy mast-o-khiar, which is a refreshing yogurt mixed with cucumbers, dried mint, and rose petals. My cousins are playing badminton in the open space nearby, and their laughter mixes with the gentle splashing of the centuries-old fountains. We do not need expensive entertainment or complicated plans because simply being together with family in this beautiful place, eating simple food, and watching the world go by is more than enough to make our hearts full of gratitude and joy.
