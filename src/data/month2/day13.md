I am standing in my grandmother's warm kitchen in Oaxaca, which is a state in southern Mexico that is famous for its indigenous cultures and incredible food traditions. The kitchen opens out to a peaceful courtyard with a blooming Jacaranda tree, and the beautiful purple flowers are falling everywhere like colorful rain. The rich smell of toasted corn fills the entire house and drifts out into the narrow cobblestone streets. I watch my grandmother take a small ball of masa, which is the traditional corn dough that Mexican people have been making for thousands of years since the ancient Aztec and Mayan civilizations. She does not use any machine because she prefers the traditional method that her own grandmother taught her many decades ago. I can hear the rhythmic sound of her hands pressing the dough flat between her palms, and each tortilla is perfectly round and thin. She throws the fresh tortilla onto the hot comal, which is a flat clay griddle that sits over the wood fire, and within seconds it puffs up with steam and develops those characteristic brown spots. The process looks simple, but it takes many years of practice to master. Eating a fresh and hot tortilla straight from the comal with just a small pinch of sea salt is better than any expensive restaurant meal I have ever tasted, and it connects me to my ancestors who ate the very same food thousands of years ago.
