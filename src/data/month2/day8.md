I am walking through the red dirt roads of Nampula, which is the largest city in northern Mozambique and is often called the Capital of the North. The air is warm and humid, and the smell of roasting cashews fills the neighborhood. Mozambique is one of the largest cashew producers in the world, and the harvest season brings the whole community together. I stop at a small stand where a woman from the Makua tribe, which is the largest ethnic group in Mozambique, is selling freshly roasted cashews. She speaks to me in Portuguese, which is the official language here because Mozambique was once a Portuguese colony. The cashews are warm and perfectly salted, and I buy a small bag to share with my friends. As I walk past the colonial era buildings with their faded yellow walls, I think about how this city blends African traditions with its Portuguese heritage.
