I am walking through the ancient stone gateway called Bab al-Yemen into the Old City of Sana'a, and I feel as though I am stepping back in time by at least a thousand years. This walled city has been continuously inhabited for more than 2,500 years, making it one of the oldest urban settlements on Earth, and UNESCO has declared it a World Heritage Site because of its extraordinary architectural and historical significance. The tall tower houses that line the narrow streets are absolutely unique in all the world, and they are built from brown mud brick decorated with elaborate white geometric patterns made from gypsum plaster. They look remarkably like gingerbread houses from a fairy tale, with their ornate windows and intricate designs. I walk slowly through the souq, which is the Arabic word for market, and I pass by small shops and stalls that have occupied the same locations for centuries. There are sacks of aromatic spices piled high in every color of the rainbow, and there are jars of golden honey collected from hives in the mountains. The complex smell is a wonderful mixture of frankincense, myrrh, and the distinctive aroma of roasted coffee husks called qishr, which Yemenis have been brewing into a traditional drink since long before coffee became popular in Europe. I am only here today to buy fresh bread called khubz from the baker who makes it in a tandoor oven heated by wood fire. However, as I walk through this living history, touching the ancient stones and breathing air scented with the same spices my ancestors knew, I feel a profound spiritual connection to the countless generations of Yemenis who walked these exact same streets before me.
