I am squeezed into the back of a brightly decorated jeepney that is stuck in the legendary traffic of Manila, and the heat inside the vehicle is almost unbearable. A jeepney is a unique Filipino form of public transportation that was originally made from American military jeeps left behind after World War II, and local craftsmen transformed these vehicles into colorful works of art on wheels. Each jeepney is uniquely decorated with chrome horses, religious images, and bold paintings of everything from superheroes to movie stars. I am sitting knee to knee with a complete stranger who is somehow managing to hold a large basket of live chickens on her lap, and the situation is both uncomfortable and wonderfully absurd. We pass our fare coins down the long line of passengers toward the driver, and this honor system of payment shows the trust that exists within Filipino society. The warm wind blows through the open windows and carries the mixed smells of rain, exhaust fumes, and food cooking at nearby street stalls. Music from the 1980s is blasting loudly from the speakers that the driver has installed, and some passengers are singing along to the familiar tunes. It is a noisy, crowded, and bumpy ride through streets lined with palm trees and concrete buildings, but as I look out at the passing scenery and feel the energy of this massive city, I can feel the heartbeat of the Philippines all around me.
