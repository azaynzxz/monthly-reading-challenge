I am standing in a spacious courtyard in the ancient city of Samarkand, and from where I stand, I can see the magnificent blue tiled domes of the Registan square shining brilliantly in the morning sunlight like enormous turquoise jewels. The Registan is one of the most stunning architectural complexes in the entire Islamic world, and its three massive madrassas have been standing here since the fifteenth century when Samarkand was one of the most important cities on the legendary Silk Road trade route that connected China to Europe. Today, however, I am not here as a tourist to admire the ancient monuments. I am here to help cook plov for a traditional Uzbek wedding celebration, and this is a tremendous honor because plov is considered the national dish of Uzbekistan and the centerpiece of all important gatherings. We are using a massive cast-iron pot called a kazan that is so large it can cook enough rice to feed over three hundred guests, and this enormous vessel sits over an open wood fire that crackles and sends sparks dancing into the air. My specific job this morning is to chop the bright orange carrots, and there are so many carrots that my arms are getting tired from the repetitive motion. The traditional recipe calls for equal weights of rice, meat, and carrots, along with plenty of cumin, coriander, and whole garlic heads that will cook slowly and become soft and sweet. We layer all of the ingredients carefully in a specific order because the technique matters as much as the ingredients themselves. First goes the meat, which is lamb in this case, then the onions and carrots, then finally the rice on top. The pot is sealed with a heavy lid, and the plov cooks slowly for several hours while the flavors meld together into something magical. The anticipation builds throughout the afternoon as the complex and savory smell fills the entire neighborhood and draws people toward the celebration. When the time finally comes to uncover the kazan and pour the steaming plov onto the giant communal platter, the golden rice glistening with oil and dotted with tender meat and soft carrots, it feels like a true celebration of unity, community, and the ancient traditions that have sustained the people of Central Asia for thousands of years.
