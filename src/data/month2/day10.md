I am waiting in a short line at a metal cart on a busy street corner in downtown Cairo, and the noise of this ancient city is beginning to wake up all around me. I can see the famous Nile River down the street if I look past the cars and buses, and I know that Egyptians have been eating breakfast along these banks for thousands of years. I am getting my morning sandwich of Ful Medames, which is a traditional dish made from fava beans that have been slowly cooked overnight in a large copper pot. The man hands me my sandwich, and it is hot and heavy with tender beans, golden olive oil, fresh lemon juice, and a generous sprinkle of cumin. The sandwich is wrapped in newspaper, which is the traditional way to serve this humble but nutritious meal. As I stand here eating with strangers and leaning against a lamp post near the centuries-old Al-Azhar Mosque, we are all equal, and we are all starting our day together with this meal that has been the breakfast of Egyptian workers and farmers for over a thousand years.
