I am waking up early in a traditional wooden kampung house in Kelantan, which is a state in northeastern Malaysia known for preserving the old Malay way of life. The morning call to prayer echoes across the village, and I can hear roosters crowing in the distance. This peaceful village setting reminds me of the famous Malaysian animated series Upin and Ipin, which follows twin brothers living in a kampung just like this one with their beloved Opah, which means grandmother in Malay. The show has taught millions of children across Southeast Asia about Malaysian culture, family values, and the importance of community. I walk outside onto the wooden porch of the rumah panggung, which is what we call a traditional raised house built on stilts to keep cool and avoid flooding during monsoon season. My neighbor Mak Cik, which is the respectful term for an older woman, is already awake and preparing nasi kerabu, which is a famous Kelantanese dish of blue-colored rice served with herbs, fish, and spicy sambal. The blue color comes from butterfly pea flowers, and it looks almost too beautiful to eat. Children from the village are running barefoot between the houses, playing traditional games like congkak and gasing, which is a spinning top game that requires great skill. This simple kampung life, where neighbors know each other by name and everyone looks out for one another, represents the heart of Malaysian culture that Upin and Ipin has helped preserve for future generations.
