The afternoon air is crisp and chilly in the Kathmandu valley today, and I can see my breath forming small white clouds as I navigate the wonderfully chaotic narrow streets of Thamel, which is the famous tourist district of Nepal's capital city. I am dodging motorbikes that seem to come from every direction at once, and I am ducking under colorful Buddhist prayer flags that are strung between the old brick buildings and flutter constantly in the mountain breeze. These prayer flags are not merely decorations because Nepali Buddhists believe that the wind carries the sacred prayers printed on the fabric to benefit all living beings. The snow-capped peaks of the mighty Himalayas are hidden behind clouds today, but I know they are there, watching over the valley as they have for millions of years before humans ever walked this earth. I stop at a small and unassuming street stall that has become my favorite place in all of Kathmandu for one specific reason: they make the best momos in the city. Momos are Nepali dumplings that were originally brought here by Tibetan refugees who crossed the high mountain passes after the Chinese occupation of their homeland, and over the decades, Nepalis have adopted and adapted this dish until it has become an essential part of our own national cuisine. The dumplings are steaming in tall metal stacker containers, and the steam rises dramatically into the cold air, carrying with it the mouthwatering smell of seasoned meat, minced onions, and aromatic spices. I order a plate of buff momos, which are made with water buffalo meat because cows are sacred in Hindu Nepal and cannot be eaten. The cook places a generous portion in front of me, and I immediately dip one of the hot dumplings into the accompanying sauce, which is a spicy tomato and sesame chutney that provides a perfect balance of heat and tang. The first bite burns my tongue slightly because I am too impatient to let it cool properly, and I have to open my mouth and fan the steam with my hand while my friends laugh at my predictable mistake. But the warmth spreads quickly through my body, and suddenly the cold mountain afternoon does not feel so harsh anymore.
