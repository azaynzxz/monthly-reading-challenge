The annual monsoon rains have flooded the low-lying fields near my village in the Sylhet division of northeastern Bangladesh, transforming the entire landscape into a vast network of shimmering wetlands that stretch as far as the eye can see. I am wading slowly into the shallow warm water, and I am holding a traditional bamboo trap called a polo that my grandfather wove with his own hands many years ago. It is remarkably quiet out here in the flooded fields, and there is just the gentle sound of rain falling on the water surface, creating thousands of tiny ripples that spread outward in ever-widening circles. In the distance, I can see the famous tea gardens of Sylhet covering the rolling hills, and they look like a beautiful green carpet draped over the land. Bangladesh is called the land of rivers because it sits in the delta of three mighty waterways, and during the monsoon season, more than one third of the entire country is covered by water. I am not fishing for sport or for a trophy to hang on my wall, but I am simply trying to catch enough small fish to feed my family for dinner. After waiting patiently for nearly an hour, I finally feel movement in my trap, and I lift it carefully to find several silvery fish flashing in the sunlight. Catching even these few small fish feels like a tremendous success because it connects me deeply to the land, the water, and the seasonal rhythms that have shaped the lives of Bengali people for thousands of years. My ancestors fished in these very same waters, and I hope my children and grandchildren will continue this tradition long after I am gone.
