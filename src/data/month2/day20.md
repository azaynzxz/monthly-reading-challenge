After a long and exhausting day of work under the hot East African sun, I pull up a worn plastic chair outside a small tea shop on a dusty side street in Hargeisa, which is the second largest city in Somalia and serves as the capital of the self-declared republic of Somaliland. The wide unpaved street in front of me is busy with an unusual mix of traffic, and I watch with amusement as camels and cars share the road, somehow managing to navigate around each other without any traffic lights or rules. I am drinking shaah, which is the traditional Somali tea that I have loved since my earliest childhood memories. This special tea is brewed extremely strong and then mixed with generous amounts of powerful spices including cardamom, cinnamon, cloves, and especially ginger, which gives it a warming heat that tingles on your tongue. The tea is always served very sweet because sugar is considered a sign of hospitality and prosperity in our culture. I sit here in the fading light of evening with several elders from my neighborhood, and I listen with respect as they recite beautiful classical poetry that has been passed down through oral tradition for hundreds of years. In Somali culture, poetry is one of the highest art forms, and skilled poets are celebrated and respected throughout society. We do not need televisions or the internet for our entertainment because we have something much more valuable. We have stories, and we have poems, and we have the wisdom of generations. And somehow, all of these ancient traditions flow more easily and taste more meaningful when accompanied by a cup of perfectly spiced tea.
