I am sitting in a long wooden boat called a klotok as it travels slowly up the Sekonyer River deep in the heart of Kalimantan, which is the Indonesian part of the island of Borneo and one of the last great wilderness areas on Earth. The ancient rainforest rises like green walls on both sides of the muddy brown river, and the air is thick with humidity and the sounds of countless birds and insects creating a symphony of jungle noise. Kalimantan is home to some of the oldest rainforests in the world, and scientists believe these forests have existed for over 130 million years, making them even older than the Amazon. Our klotok moves at a gentle pace because we are searching for orangutans, which are the great red apes that live only on the islands of Borneo and Sumatra. The name orangutan comes from the Malay words orang hutan, which means person of the forest, and local people have always treated these intelligent creatures with respect. Suddenly, our guide points excitedly to a tree near the riverbank, and I see a mother orangutan with her baby clinging to her chest, watching us with curious eyes from high in the branches. The Dayak people, who are the indigenous tribes of Borneo, have lived in harmony with this forest for thousands of years, and their traditional longhouses can still be found in remote villages along the river. As the sun begins to set and paints the sky in shades of orange and purple, I realize that I am witnessing something precious and fragile, a wilderness that we must protect for future generations to experience and appreciate.
