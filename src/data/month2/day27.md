I am staying at a beautiful old house in the village of Ribáuè in Nampula Province of northern Mozambique, and this building belongs to my company which purchased it to house employees working in the region. The house was constructed by the Portuguese more than a century ago, and its thick stone walls are painted a warm yellow color that has weathered many decades of tropical sun and monsoon rain. From the covered porch, I can see the magnificent Mount M'pàluwé rising in the distance like a sleeping giant covered in a thick green blanket of forest. The locals sometimes call this mountain Ampleve, and it is the most famous natural feature in the entire region because its dramatic rocky peaks rise sharply from the flat surrounding plains and can be seen from many kilometers away. I have come here with a plan to climb to the summit because the views from the top are supposed to be extraordinary. To reach the base of the mountain, I will need to cross a lake using a traditional wooden boat that is paddled by hand, and I feel genuinely excited about this adventure as I imagine gliding across the calm water toward those towering granite peaks. However, nature has other plans for me today. Dark clouds are gathering quickly over the mountain, and I can smell the rain coming in the humid air that grows heavier with each passing minute. The first drops begin to fall, and within seconds, the sky opens up with the kind of tropical downpour that makes you understand why Mozambique has such lush vegetation during the rainy season. I retreat to the safety of the hundred-year-old house and sit on the porch, watching the rain fall in sheets across the village. The downpour continues for hours, and I watch as the mountain disappears completely behind curtains of grey rain. I will not climb Mount M'pàluwé today, but I have discovered something perhaps more valuable: a quiet moment in a village where the past and present exist together in harmony, where Portuguese architecture stands beside African traditions, and where the rhythm of life moves slowly enough to appreciate both the mountains and the rain.
