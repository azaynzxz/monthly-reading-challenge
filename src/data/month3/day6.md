I am sitting on the floor of my family's workshop in Herat, a city in western Afghanistan known for its centuries-old tradition of carpet making. A large wooden loom stands in front of me, strung with hundreds of white cotton threads. My grandmother sits beside me, guiding my hands as I tie tiny knots of dyed wool around each thread. The carpet we are making is called a Qalin, and it follows a geometric pattern in deep reds and dark blues. She tells me that each pattern has a meaning, and some designs have been passed down for five or six generations. The wool smells earthy, and the room is quiet except for the soft clicking of our knives cutting the thread after each knot. One carpet can take six months to complete, so patience is the most important skill. My grandmother always says that every knot carries a prayer for the family who will eventually sit on it. This craft is how our people have told their stories for hundreds of years.
