I am sitting by the window of a small wooden guesthouse in Malam Jabba, which is a hill station nestled in the Hindu Kush mountains of Khyber Pakhtunkhwa, the northwestern province of Pakistan. Outside, the entire valley has turned white. The northern zone of KP, meaning everything from the Koh Hindu Kush range down to the valley of Peshawar, receives heavy snowfall each winter, and today is no different. Thick snow covers the tall Deodar cedar and pine trees, bending their branches under the weight. The mountains behind them look enormous and silent. I hold a cup of Kashmiri chai, called Noon Chai here, which is a pink tea made with salt, milk, and crushed almonds. It warms my chest with every sip. Children outside are throwing snowballs and sliding down slopes on plastic sheets, their laughter echoing across the valley. Malam Jabba also has a ski resort, making it one of the very few places in South Asia where people can ski. Looking at these peaceful white peaks, I feel grateful to witness this magical and often overlooked side of Pakistan.
