I am standing on the white sandy shore of Tanjung Bira in Bulukumba, South Sulawesi, watching a team of master builders shape the hull of a Phinisi. A Phinisi is a traditional two-masted wooden sailing ship, and the Konjo people of this coast have built them for centuries. The air smells of fresh sawdust and ocean salt. What amazes me is that these builders work without any blueprints or written plans. They measure everything by eye and hand, relying on knowledge passed from father to son. The wood they use is ironwood, called Ulin, which is so dense and strong that it sinks in water. Hammers ring out in a steady rhythm as planks are fitted together with wooden pegs. In 2017, UNESCO recognized this boatbuilding tradition as a Masterpiece of Intangible Cultural Heritage. Watching these men turn raw timber into a ship that will sail across oceans makes me proud of our ancestral wisdom.
